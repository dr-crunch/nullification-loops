The Central Justiciable Question
Placed before Judge Eugene Regan of the Court of Justice of the European Union on March 9th, 2025. No response received.
CJEU · Primary Legal Question · Unanswered
"Can a Member State lawfully hold the Council Presidency while suppressing an ethics complaint that documents its own breach of Treaty obligations in areas within the Presidency's functional responsibilities?"
No appropriate response has been received — no explanation postulated.
Core Proposition · All 27 Member States
"Can a Member State indefinitely avoid acting on risks it has acknowledged, without legal or institutional consequence?"
If the answer is yes — whether through judicial silence, Commission inaction, or parliamentary deflection — then a new norm has been established: acknowledgment discharges obligation. It does not. But in the absence of a determination to the contrary, it functions as if it does.

Summary of Legal Issues · Four Pillars
Four Independent Legal Issues
Each standing independently and each compounding the others. The DFA is the originating failure that every downstream institution has been recruited to protect.
Issue I
The Conversion Tort
The Anchor · Strict Liability
26 kg of proprietary O.R. materials, delivered in 2006, have never been returned, evaluated, rejected, or compensated. State retention without lawful basis, without compensation, and without determination is the foundational injury. Conversion is a strict liability tort in Irish law. Every subsequent administrative act that prevents resolution compounds it. The Clerk's office, by assuming custody of the complaint about those materials and refusing to transmit it, became a proximate participant in the continuation of the conversion.
Issue II
The Nullification Loop
Structural Non-Governance
The JCEUA-I-57 declination followed by deletion of correspondence is the sharpest single instance. Deletion after declination — without transmission to a competent body, without statutory account of inapplicability — is the Silence Veto operating at its most consequential. The record is not merely unanswered; it is actively removed. Five-stage closed system: Acknowledgement → Deferral → Remit Displacement → Sufficiency Assertion → Record Suppression / Reset. Personnel-independent across six Taoisigh.
Issue III
The McGrath Dimension
Constitutional Contradiction
Michael McGrath was formally notified during the Community Call of March 2020 as a sitting TD. He is now European Commissioner for Democracy, Justice, the Rule of Law and Consumer Protection — the office specifically empowered to initiate infringement proceedings against Member States for rule-of-law failures. The Commission cannot credibly assess Ireland's rule-of-law compliance in the context of the Council Presidency while its own Commissioner for that portfolio has an undischarged notice in his personal parliamentary record. This is not a rhetorical point; it is a jurisdictional one.
Issue IV
Ultra Vires Gatekeeping
Clerks' Procedure · Wednesbury
The Clerk applied criteria — persuasion, systemic framing, policy characterisation — that have no basis in the Ethics in Public Office Act 1995 or the relevant Standing Orders. A decision made on a criterion that does not exist in the enabling statute is not merely incorrect; it is ultra vires and susceptible to judicial review on Wednesbury irrationality grounds. The fait accompli claim — that Section 8(3) decisions admit no rebuttal — is constitutionally unsupportable under Article 40.3. The inverted audi alteram partem (hearing the complainant, ruling against them, refusing to notify the named members) is a standalone natural justice violation.

Part V · Political Cartography · Member State Reception
Six Categories of Member State Reception
The precedent is not received uniformly across the Union. What follows is a strategic map of where the unanswered question exerts pressure, and how.
Category 1
The Founding Federalists
France · Germany · Italy · Belgium · Luxembourg · Netherlands
These are the states for whom the EU's rule-of-law architecture is constitutive investment. The problem is morphological: the nullification loop presents as procedural normality rather than overt defiance. The enforcement vocabulary of Article 7 TEU was designed for confrontation — it is less well calibrated for evasion conducted through formal compliance.
Pressure: Art. 7 vocabulary gap · Kelsenian tradition · Conseil d'État
Category 2
The Nordic-Baltic Cluster
Sweden · Finland · Denmark · Estonia · Latvia · Lithuania
High-trust governance traditions, strong ombudsman institutions. Remit displacement, acknowledgment-as-closure: any Nordic administrative lawyer recognises these as precisely the failure modes their oversight systems are designed to prevent. Lithuania is the second member of Ireland's incoming Council Trio — a natural partner audience. Estonia's digital governance sensitivity to data sovereignty is acute.
Pressure: Ombudsman jurisdiction gap · Trio coherence · Estonian data sovereignty
Category 3
The Visegrád Group
Poland · Hungary · Czechia · Slovakia
Most internally differentiated category. Poland under Tusk has direct interest in enforcement credibility — compliance has political cost. Hungary's government knows resistance via procedural courtesy is more durable than confrontation. Czech civil society is alert to institutional capture. Slovakia understands the blunt argument: if Ireland can run the loop without consequence, so can Bratislava.
Pressure: Bifurcated — pro-EU and sovereigntist framings both viable
Category 4
The Southern Arc
Spain · Portugal · Greece · Cyprus
Each emerged from authoritarian governance within living memory. For all four, the EU's rule-of-law framework is part of the scaffolding of democratic transition. Cyprus is Ireland's immediate Presidency predecessor — its government officials have current operational knowledge. Greece holds the Presidency in 2027 as the third Trio member; a compromised first Trio member creates reputational complications that are not Ireland's alone to manage.
Pressure: Trio coherence · Cyprus proximity · Democratic transition stakes
Category 5
Newer Accession States
Bulgaria · Romania · Croatia · Slovenia
This cluster encounters the precedent through structural asymmetry. Bulgaria and Romania have been held to a standard of procedural compliance — externally monitored — that older member states demonstrably evade through more sophisticated means. Romania's civil society has been among the most mobilised in Europe against precisely this kind of procedural capture. The parallel between Romania's domestic nullification mechanisms and the Irish loop is analytically precise.
Pressure: Monitoring asymmetry · Romanian civil society · Accession-promise gap
Category 6 · Cross-Cutting
The Data Sovereignty Cluster
Germany · France · Austria · Estonia · All 27 via DPC
Every EU citizen whose personal data is processed by a US technology company regulated through Ireland is a stakeholder. Germany, France, and Austria have invested legal and diplomatic capital in EU data sovereignty through Safe Harbour, Privacy Shield, and the Data Privacy Framework. The Irish case puts that investment at risk. The question is not whether Ireland possesses formal regulatory authority — it does. The question is whether sovereignty exercised without demonstrable analytical capacity is sovereignty in substance.
Pressure: DPC credibility · Safe Harbour / Privacy Shield investment · Berlin · Paris · Vienna · Tallinn
Part IV · The Precedent Argument · Every Member State
What the Precedent Permits
Should Ireland's twenty-year nullification loop remain unchallenged and unresolved, the following propositions become institutionally defensible for any member state.
A
A state may receive a formal submission engaging dual-use obligations under Regulation 428/2009, acknowledge its receipt, and take no evaluative action — indefinitely. The Irish record from November 11th, 2006 through February 27th, 2026 demonstrates this is operationally possible without triggering infringement proceedings, CJEU referral, or Commission intervention.
S-exportable · Reg. 428/2009
B
A state may deploy a procedural nullification loop — serial jurisdictional deflection across departments, committees, and constitutional bodies — as a substitute for substantive decision-making. Five distinct discourse clusters documented in Memorandum E. The system produces the outcome without requiring the intent.
S-structural · Five discourse clusters
C
A state may assert that its administrative determinations are "final" and "admit no rebuttal" — even where those determinations suppress evidence, deny reasons, and prevent transmission of a complaint to the members it concerns — without those assertions being tested. The Clerk of Dáil Éireann made precisely this claim on September 10th, 2025. If it stands, it stands for every equivalent officer in every member state parliament.
S-suppressed · Ultra vires gatekeeping
D
A state may hold the Presidency of the Council of the European Union while suppressing an ethics complaint documenting its own breach of Treaty obligations in areas within the Presidency's functional responsibilities. This is not a hypothetical. It is the position Ireland will occupy from July 1st, 2026. If no mechanism intervenes before that date, the precedent is set in the most visible institutional context available.
