## Part IV â€” The Precedent Argument: Every Member State

### I. The Core Proposition

The Irish case is, on its surface, a domestic matter â€” a citizen's research, a government's inaction, a parliamentary system's procedural evasion. But the legal question it generates is not domestic at all. It is a question about the outer boundary of sovereign discretion within a supranational legal order, and its answer binds every member state equally.

The question, as put before Judge Eugene Regan of the Court of Justice of the European Union on March 9th, 2025, and not answered, is this:

> **Can a Member State indefinitely avoid acting on risks it has acknowledged, without legal or institutional consequence?**

If the answer is yes â€” whether through judicial silence, Commission inaction, or parliamentary deflection â€” then a new norm has been established, not by treaty, not by legislation, not by judgment, but by the accumulated weight of institutional non-response. That norm is: **acknowledgment discharges obligation**.

It does not. But in the absence of a determination to the contrary, it functions as if it does. And that functional equivalence is exportable to every capital in the Union.

---

### II. What the Precedent Permits

Should Ireland's twenty-year nullification loop remain unchallenged and unresolved, the following propositions become institutionally defensible for any member state:

**A state may receive a formal submission engaging dual-use obligations under Regulation 428/2009, acknowledge its receipt, and take no evaluative action â€” indefinitely.** The Irish record from November 11th, 2006 through February 27th, 2026 demonstrates this is operationally possible without triggering infringement proceedings, CJEU referral, or Commission intervention.

**A state may deploy a procedural nullification loop â€” serial jurisdictional deflection across departments, committees, and constitutional bodies â€” as a substitute for substantive decision-making.** Memorandum E documents five distinct discourse clusters by which this is achieved without any single actor being individually accountable. The system produces the outcome without requiring the intent. Any state with a sufficiently complex bureaucratic architecture can replicate it.

**A state may assert that its administrative determinations are "final" and "admit no rebuttal" â€” even where those determinations suppress evidence, deny reasons, and prevent transmission of a complaint to the members it concerns â€” without those assertions being tested.** The Clerk of DÃ¡il Ã‰ireann made precisely this claim on September 10th, 2025. It was rebutted in constitutional terms on September 24th, October 30th, and December 12th, 2025. It has not been answered. If it stands, it stands for every equivalent officer in every member state parliament.

**A state may hold the Presidency of the Council of the European Union while suppressing an ethics complaint documenting its own breach of Treaty obligations in areas within the Presidency's functional responsibilities.** This is not a hypothetical. It is the position Ireland will occupy from July 1st, 2026. If no mechanism intervenes before that date, the precedent is set in the most visible institutional context available.

### III. The Exportability of the Nullification Loop

The nullification loop as documented in this case is not an Irish invention. It is a structural property of any administrative system in which: oversight functions are distributed across multiple bodies; no single body has a positive duty to receive cross-cutting matters; and acknowledgment can be operationalised as closure.

These conditions exist in every member state. The five discourse clusters identified in Memorandum E â€” remit displacement, acknowledgment-as-closure, deferral without destination, sufficiency assertion, and record suppression â€” are not culturally specific. They are generic bureaucratic defence mechanisms available to any government that wishes to deploy them.

What makes the Irish case significant is not that it happened in Ireland. It is that it happened _durably_ â€” over twenty years, across four governments, through two EU Council terms, and in full documentary view â€” without triggering any corrective mechanism at the domestic or supranational level. That durability is the proof of concept. It demonstrates that the loop is stable under sustained challenge. A state that observes the Irish record now knows that the loop can be run to completion.

### V. The Contagion Mechanism

The precedent does not spread through explicit imitation. No government minister in Warsaw, Budapest, Prague, or Bratislava â€” or Rome, or The Hague, or Stockholm â€” will announce that they have adopted the Irish model. The spread is structural and silent. It occurs through three channels:

**Institutional learning.** Bureaucratic systems observe what works. Legal counsel across member state administrations tracks EU enforcement patterns. When a pattern of non-enforcement becomes visible â€” when a state is observed to have run a nullification loop to completion without consequence â€” that information enters the institutional knowledge base of every comparable administration. It becomes available as precedent in the next internal legal memo advising a minister on whether a formal submission requires a substantive response.

**Litigation strategy.** In future cases brought before the CJEU involving member state inaction on acknowledged obligations, the Irish record will be discoverable. If Ireland prevails through silence â€” whether because no judgment was sought, or because a judgment was sought and not obtained â€” that absence of adverse determination is itself a form of precedent. It narrows the space for future claimants and widens the space for future respondent states.

**Commission credibility.** The Commission's capacity to enforce rule-of-law standards depends partly on the credibility of the threat of enforcement. If it is seen to tolerate a documented, twenty-year, publicly indexed nullification loop in a member state that is simultaneously appointed to lead the Council, its credibility as an enforcement actor is measurably diminished â€” not only in Ireland but in every capital that observes the outcome.

### VI. The Inversion: Why the Precedent Argument Serves Every Member State

The argument cuts equally against complacency on both sides of the EU's internal political divide.

For **pro-EU, rule-of-law-committed** member states â€” France, Germany, the Netherlands, the Nordic states, the Baltic states â€” the Irish precedent is a threat to the architecture they have invested in. The rule of law is not self-executing. It depends on mechanisms that are triggered, on complaints that are processed, on obligations that are enforced. If those mechanisms can be neutralised by a sophisticated enough nullification loop, the architecture is weaker than it appears.

For **sovereigntist, Eurosceptic** member states â€” Hungary, Slovakia, and, on certain issues, Czechia and Poland â€” the Irish precedent is a double-edged instrument. On one hand, it demonstrates that domestic procedural resistance to supranational pressure is viable. On the other, it demonstrates that the same vulnerability exists for them: if Ireland's obligations to its own citizens can be nullified by institutional procedure, so can their citizens' rights under EU law. A governance architecture that can be defeated by the state can also be weaponised by it â€” and the V4 states have lived experience of what that means.

The precedent argument is therefore not a narrow Irish grievance dressed in universal language. It is a genuine structural question about the outer limit of member state sovereignty in a supranational legal order â€” a question that every government, every parliament, every constitutional court, and every citizen in the European Union has a stake in seeing answered.

---

### VII. The Unanswered Question

The question put to Judge Eugene Regan on March 9th, 2025 remains open. No response was received. That silence is itself a datum â€” not a determination, but an absence of determination, which is a different and more dangerous thing. A determination, even an adverse one, closes a question. An absence of determination leaves it structurally open, available to be answered in practice by whoever moves next.

The only variable, as the corpus states, is whether the question is resolved proactively or exposed reactively. The documentation is public. The record is indexed. The argument is made. What has not yet happened is an institutional actor â€” at the Commission, at the CJEU, in a national parliament, in a V4 chamber, in a press room â€” deciding to treat the question as requiring an answer.

These are the states for whom the EU's rule-of-law architecture is not merely a constraint but a constitutive investment. They built it. Their legal traditions â€” Kelsenian constitutionalism in Germany, the Conseil d'Ã‰tat tradition in France, the codified administrative law frameworks of the Benelux states â€” are the intellectual foundations on which Articles 2 and 19 TEU were constructed. For this group the Irish case presents a specific and uncomfortable problem. The nullification loop as documented here does not look like the rule-of-law failures they have previously confronted â€” the open defiance of an OrbÃ¡n, the constitutional tribunal capture of a KaczyÅ„ski-era Poland. ### Category 2 â€” The Nordic-Baltic Cluster: Sweden, Finland, Denmark, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania

This is the group for whom rule-of-law is not primarily a constitutional abstraction but an operational commitment â€” grounded in high-trust governance traditions, low corruption indices, and a strong domestic expectation that public institutions process lawful input and discharge acknowledged obligations as a matter of administrative hygiene, not political choice. In Sweden, Finland, and Denmark, the ombudsman institution is genuinely powerful. In Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania â€” all post-Soviet states that rebuilt their administrative architectures from near-zero after 1991 â€” procedural integrity is a hard-won value, not an inherited one.

For this cluster, the Irish case is legible at first reading. The five discourse clusters documented in Memorandum E â€” remit displacement, acknowledgment-as-closure, deferral without destination, sufficiency assertion, record suppression â€” would be recognised immediately by any Nordic administrative lawyer as a failure mode that their own oversight mechanisms are specifically designed to prevent. The Finnish Parliamentary Ombudsman and the Swedish Justitieombudsman both have jurisdictions that extend precisely to identifying when an institution has substituted procedural form for substantive decision-making. That these instruments do not exist at EU level â€” that there is no supranational ombudsman with jurisdiction over member state administrative conduct of this kind â€” is itself an argument that this cluster is well-positioned to make.

Lithuania is a special case within the cluster. As the second member of Ireland's incoming Trio (January 1st to June 30th, 2027), Lithuania will be inside the EU's governance architecture during the period when the nullification loop's most acute Presidency-era consequences are still live. Lithuanian civil society and the Lithuanian Seimas have an institutional interest in the credibility of the Trio's shared governance programme that makes them a natural audience for the precedent argument â€” not as a hostile party to Ireland's Presidency, but as a partner state that has its own reputational stake in the Trio's coherence.

Estonia deserves separate mention for a different reason. As the EU's most digitally advanced governance state â€” the architect of e-Residency, digital identity infrastructure, and paperless public administration â€” Estonia has a particular sensitivity to the argument that the Irish case engages around data sovereignty and the systematic failure to apply O.R.-based risk assessment to complex data transfers. The Irish Data Protection Commission's claimed sovereignty over EU citizens' data, exercised without the analytical rigour that O.R. governance would require, is an argument that resonates directly in Tallinn, where the architecture of digital trust is taken more seriously than anywhere else in the Union.

### Category 3 â€” The VisegrÃ¡d Group: Poland, Hungary, Czechia, Slovakia

The V4 is the most internally differentiated of all categories, and the most politically charged. It requires the most careful navigation, because the same precedent argument reads differently depending on which side of the V4's internal fault line you are addressing.

Poland under Donald Tusk is categorically different from Poland under the Law and Justice governments that preceded him. Tusk's administration has invested heavily in demonstrating its rule-of-law credibility to Brussels â€” undoing the judicial reforms that had generated Article 7 proceedings, restoring public broadcaster independence, and reorienting Polish foreign policy toward the mainstream EU position on Ukraine. For this Poland, the Irish precedent argument is an ally, not a threat. A Polish government that has paid the political cost of restoring rule-of-law compliance has a direct interest in ensuring that compliance is not made optional by a precedent of successful evasion. The argument that Ireland's nullification loop must be challenged â€” that the Commission must demonstrate it will enforce compliance even when the evasion is polite and procedurally dressed â€” is an argument that serves Warsaw's own institutional narrative.

Hungary under Viktor OrbÃ¡n is the inverse case. Hungary's relationship with the EU's rule-of-law architecture is one of sustained, deliberate, and largely successful attrition â€” using the instruments of democratic procedure to hollow out the substance of democratic accountability, while maintaining enough formal compliance to delay or dilute enforcement consequences. OrbÃ¡n's government has direct experience of what happens when a state resists the Commission openly, and considerable experience of how long that resistance can be sustained. The Irish case adds a new data point that Fidesz strategists will have noted: that resistance need not be confrontational to be effective. A nullification loop maintained through procedural courtesy for twenty years, in a state with a strong democratic reputation, has achieved more durable evasion than Hungary's more visible confrontations have managed. This is not a lesson that should go unacknowledged. The argument for engaging Hungary on this precedent is paradoxically strongest precisely because Budapest understands it â€” and because a Hungarian government that has been the _subject_ of rule-of-law pressure is well-positioned to argue that the Commission's selective enforcement undermines the credibility of the framework it claims to uphold.

Czechia under Andrej BabiÅ¡ and with Tomio Okamura as President of the Chamber of Deputies since November 5th, 2025 sits in a complex position. Okamura's SPD is explicitly sovereigntist and Eurosceptic, and his active pursuit of V4 parliamentary cooperation â€” first official visit to Bratislava, Budapest engagement planned â€” positions him as a node in a parliamentary network that is institutionally sceptical of Brussels enforcement. But the Czech constitutional tradition is also genuinely strong, and Czech civil society â€” which mounted sustained resistance to BabiÅ¡'s conflicts of interest and Okamura's earlier political interventions â€” is among the most alert in Central Europe to precisely the kind of institutional capture that the nullification loop represents. The precedent argument therefore has two distinct entry points in Prague: the parliamentary-sovereigntist channel, for whom the argument is that Commission selective enforcement is itself a rule-of-law failure; and the civil-society channel, for whom the argument is that the loop is a template for domestic capture that Czech institutions should recognise and resist.

Slovakia under Robert Fico is the most aligned with the Hungarian posture of the four V4 states. Fico's government has moved sharply toward a pro-Russian, anti-Brussels orientation since returning to power, and his administration has its own rule-of-law concerns. The precedent argument here is blunt: if Ireland can run a nullification loop to completion without consequence, so can Slovakia, and the Commission's credibility as an enforcement actor is diminished accordingly. This is an argument that Bratislava will understand without requiring elaboration.

### Category 4 â€” The Southern Arc: Spain, Portugal, Greece, Cyprus

These states share a specific historical experience that gives the precedent argument a particular texture: each emerged from authoritarian governance within living memory â€” Spain and Portugal from the 1970s, Greece somewhat earlier but with a military junta lasting until 1974, Cyprus with its own constitutional rupture of 1974 still structurally unresolved. For all four, the EU's rule-of-law framework is not an abstract legal architecture. It is part of the scaffolding of democratic transition â€” the external guarantee that made domestic reform credible and irreversible.

This historical investment cuts sharply against complacency about the Irish precedent. If acknowledgment-as-closure becomes the operative norm, the external guarantee that these states relied upon â€” the proposition that supranational law creates obligations that domestic political convenience cannot simply defer â€” is weakened for every future democratic transition that seeks to anchor itself to EU membership. The argument is not that Spain, Portugal, Greece, or Cyprus are themselves at risk of reverting to authoritarian governance. It is that the architecture they helped justify and whose protection they once needed is being hollowed quietly, in a member state that nobody would identify as at risk â€” and that this quiet hollowing is the most dangerous form, because it proceeds without triggering any alarm.

Cyprus holds a particular position within this group. As the current holder of the Council Presidency until June 30th, 2026 â€” the immediate predecessor to Ireland's term â€” Cyprus is the state most directly adjacent to the Presidency conflict-of-interest question. It is also a member of Ireland's incoming Trio's predecessor group (Poland, Denmark, Cyprus), which means its government officials have direct knowledge of the handover process and the state of the Council's agenda as it passes to Dublin. Nicosia is therefore a natural first point of contact for the Presidency-framing of the argument, precisely because Cyprus understands from current operational experience what it means to hold that role â€” and what it would mean to hold it while suppressing an acknowledged governance failure.

Greece will hold the Presidency in the second half of 2027 â€” as the third member of the Ireland/Lithuania/Greece Trio. Athens therefore has an institutional interest in the coherence and credibility of the Trio's shared programme that mirrors Lithuania's. A Trio whose first member is holding the Presidency while suppressing an ethics complaint creates reputational and operational complications for the Trio as a whole. Greece's current government under Kyriakos Mitsotakis is strongly pro-EU and has invested in positioning Greece as a credible, reforming partner state â€” an investment that is not served by association with a Presidency that is visibly compromised.

### Category 5 â€” The New Accession States and Candidate Adjacents: Bulgaria, Romania, Croatia, Slovenia

These states occupy a distinctive position in relation to the precedent because they are the most recently integrated and, in several cases, still subject to monitoring mechanisms â€” the Cooperation and Verification Mechanism in the case of Bulgaria â€” that presuppose a higher level of external scrutiny than older member states face. The structural asymmetry this creates is directly relevant to the precedent argument: if a state that has been subject to intensive rule-of-law monitoring for years is held to a standard of procedural compliance that an older member state can evade through a sophisticated nullification loop, the monitoring architecture itself is exposed as selective.

Romania is the most significant state in this group for a specific reason: it has its own deep experience of institutional resistance to accountability â€” the DNA (National Anticorruption Directorate) battles, the repeated attempts to decriminalise political corruption through emergency ordinance, the long pattern of formal compliance masking substantive evasion. Romanian civil society, which has been among the most mobilised in Europe against precisely this kind of procedural capture, is a natural audience for the Memoranda. The parallel between Romania's domestic nullification mechanisms and the Irish loop is not a comfortable one, but it is analytically precise â€” and Romanian lawyers and civil-society actors have the vocabulary and the experience to engage with it on exactly the right terms.

Croatia and Slovenia, as the most recently acceded full members, bring the freshest perspective on what EU membership was supposed to guarantee. For both states, the promise of accession included â€” explicitly and procedurally â€” a commitment to governance standards that EU membership would both require and protect. The Irish case challenges that promise from an unexpected direction: not by showing that a new member state has failed to meet the standard, but by showing that an old member state has evaded it, without consequence, for twenty years.

### Category 6 â€” The Data Sovereignty Cluster: A Cross-Cutting Category

This final category cuts across all the others, because the data sovereignty dimension of the Irish case is not geographically bounded â€” it is structurally universal. Every EU citizen whose personal data is processed by a US technology company headquartered or regulated through Ireland is a stakeholder in the question of whether the Irish DPC is exercising genuine, O.R.-informed, evidence-based oversight â€” or whether the sovereignty claim Ireland makes on behalf of the EU is, as the corpus describes it, a legal fiction.

The states with the strongest political interest in this question are those whose governments have been most active in challenging US data surveillance practices â€” notably Germany, France, and Austria, all of which have been parties to or amicus participants in the major data transfer cases that led to the successive collapses of Safe Harbour, Privacy Shield, and the current fragility of the Data Privacy Framework. These states have invested legal, political, and diplomatic capital in the proposition that EU data sovereignty is real and enforceable. The Irish case puts that investment at risk â€” not by challenging the legal framework, but by demonstrating that the regulatory capacity claimed in its name may be systematically underresourced in the precise analytical domain that would be needed to make it real.

This is a cross-cutting argument that reaches pro-EU federalists in Berlin and Paris, digital governance specialists in Tallinn and Amsterdam, and legal practitioners in Vienna and Brussels alike. It requires no agreement with the Operations Research methodology to find it compelling. It requires only the recognition that regulatory claims must be backed by analytical capacity â€” and that the documented twenty-year exclusion of O.R. from Irish governance is evidence that the capacity is not there.

### Synthesis

Mapped across these six categories, the precedent argument has a different face in every capital â€” but it is the same argument. Whether the entry point is doctrinal (for the Founding Federalists), administrative (for the Nordic-Baltic cluster), political (for the V4), historical (for the Southern Arc), structural (for the newer accession states), or technical (for the data sovereignty cluster), the underlying proposition is identical: the question of whether a member state may indefinitely acknowledge an obligation without discharging it has not been answered, and every state in the Union â€” from whichever political tradition it approaches the question â€” has a stake in the answer.

The Presidency framing expires on December 31st, 2026. This argument does not expire. It is permanent, structural, and addressed to the Union as a whole.
