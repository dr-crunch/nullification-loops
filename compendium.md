## Part I ‚Äî The Council Presidency: Legal Basis and Structural Context

### I. Legal Basis and Governance

The rotation of the Presidency is governed by **Article 16(9)** of the Treaty on European Union (TEU) and **Article 236** of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU). The current specific order was established by **Council Decision (EU) 2016/1316**, which revised the schedule through December 31st, 2030, following the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union.

### II. Rotation Schedule (2024‚Äì2030)

The Presidency rotates every six months. As of February 27th, 2026, the Presidency is held by **Cyprus**.

| **Year** | **January ‚Äì June** | **July ‚Äì December** |
|---|---|---|
| **2024** | Belgium | Hungary |
| **2025** | Poland | Denmark |
| **2026** | **Cyprus** (Current) | **Ireland** (Upcoming) |
| **2027** | Lithuania | Greece |
| **2028** | Italy | Latvia |
| **2029** | Luxembourg | Netherlands |
| **2030** | Slovakia | Malta |

> **Note:** Per Article 3 of Decision 2016/1316, the Council must determine the order for the period beginning January 1st, 2031, no later than December 31st, 2029.

### III. The "Trio" System

To ensure continuity across the six-month terms, Member States work in groups of three, known as **"Trios."** These trios prepare a shared 18-month programme of long-term goals and a common agenda.

- **Current Trio (2025‚Äì2026):** Poland, Denmark, and Cyprus.
- **Next Trio (2026‚Äì2027):** Ireland, Lithuania, and Greece.

### IV. Functional Responsibilities of the Presidency

The Member State holding the Presidency is responsible for:

1. **Chairing Meetings** ‚Äî leading sessions of the Council (except the Foreign Affairs Council, which is chaired by the High Representative).
2. **Agenda Setting** ‚Äî determining the priority of legislative files and brokering compromises between Member States.
3. **Representation** ‚Äî representing the Council in negotiations with other EU institutions, such as the European Parliament and the Commission (trilogues).

## Part II ‚Äî Structural Entrapment: The Presidency as Accountability Dissolution Mechanism

### A. The Temporal Trap

The convergence of Ireland's forthcoming Presidency with the twenty-year suppression of the O.R. ethics complaint constitutes what the documentation characterises as **structural entrapment** ‚Äî a condition in which institutional architecture systematically prevents the discharge of accountability obligations.

- Ireland assumes the EU Council Presidency: **July 1st, 2026** (in approximately four months from the date of this document)
- Ethics complaint suppression: **November 11th, 2006 ‚Äì February 27th, 2026** (twenty years)

**The Nullification Loop in temporal terms**: each six-month rotation creates a "responsibility sink" where incoming presidencies inherit no institutional memory and outgoing presidencies transfer no accountability. The 18-month Trio system compounds this: Poland, Denmark, and Cyprus (the current Trio) have no contact with Ireland's domestic governance failures; Ireland, Lithuania, and Greece (the next Trio) will be too institutionally preoccupied with Council duties to audit Ireland's own record.

**The Strategic Window**: Ireland will hold the Presidency **while the ethics complaint remains suppressed** ‚Äî creating a conflict of structural interest of the highest order:

- Ireland will chair Council meetings on civil protection coordination (TFEU Art. 196)
- Ireland will broker compromises on dual-use export controls (Reg. 428/2009)
- Ireland will represent the Council in trilogues with the Parliament and Commission
- All while concealing its own twenty-year breach of those same obligations

### B. The Trio System as Loose Architecture

**Article 3, Council Decision 2016/1316** requires Trios to prepare a **shared 18-month programme** of long-term goals and a common agenda.

For the Ireland/Lithuania/Greece Trio (2026‚Äì2027):

- **July‚ÄìDecember 2026**: Ireland sets the agenda
- **January‚ÄìJune 2027**: Lithuania chairs; Ireland remains inside the Trio
- **July‚ÄìDecember 2027**: Greece chairs; Ireland remains inside the Trio

For 18 months, Ireland will be **within the governance architecture** while simultaneously: suppressing its own ethics complaint; setting EU priorities on emergency preparedness; coordinating cross-border intelligence cooperation; and ‚Äî per the structural logic of the nullification loop ‚Äî effectively blocking any Commission investigation into its own TFEU Art. 196 breach.

This is not administrative failure. It is structural entrapment without functional self-discipline.

**Irish Citizens** are the primary and foundational stakeholder. Every Irish citizen is affected by the downstream consequences of O.R. exclusion from public governance: suboptimal resource allocation in healthcare, housing, transport, asylum processing, emergency management, and national security. The citizen's standing is constitutional ‚Äî Article 40.3 of Bunreacht na h√âireann guarantees the right to have one's rights vindicated by the State. **EU Citizens Across All Member States** share risk in a deeper structural sense: Ireland's handling of EU data transfers, dual-use research, and rule-of-law obligations is not a matter of local concern. What one member state does ‚Äî or fails to do ‚Äî within the single market reverberates across shared commercial, security, and legal frameworks. The legal question posed to the CJEU is precisely this: if a member state may indefinitely acknowledge a risk without acting upon it, every member state may do the same, and the architecture of shared accountability collapses. All twenty-seven member states share risk and commerce and bear economic consequence in common.
